How do you write meaningful objectives?

Here is a SMART framework you can refer to. An effective objective should be:

  1. Specific: target a specific area for actions or improvements. e.g. What do you want to accomplish? Why is the goal important? What are the things involved?
  2. Measurable: quantify or at least suggest an indicator of progress. How much? How many? e.g. How will I know when it is accomplished?
  3. Assignable: specify who will do it
  4. Realistic: state what results can realistically be achieved, given available resources.
  5. Time-related: specify when the results can be achieved. e.g. When? What can I do six weeks from now? What can I do today?

This can be an example of a SMART goal:

Doran, G. T. (1981). There’s a SMART way to write management’s goals and objectives. Management Review, 70(11), 35–36.

The effective use of study environment is essential to academic success. Related techniques include things like how to avoid disturbance, find an appropriate study area and so on. Below listed some commonly used strategies:

  1. Use a regular study area. Students are more time efficient when they study in a place that is well-lighted, free from noise and distractions, and conducive to attention. Some students find a library a good place to study, whereas other students can create an effective study area at home.
  2. Prioritize tasks. When students have many activities, they should prioritize those that need to be completed first. In general, it is better to study difficult subjects before studying easy ones because one’s attention is usually better at the outset.
  3. Learn to say no to distractions. When friends, siblings, or others want to talk instead of study or to skip studying entirely, students must be prepared to say no in a way that is not offensive.

Zimmerman, B. J., Bonner, S., & Kovach, R. (1996). Goal 2: Developing time planning and management skills. In Developing self-regulated learners: Beyond achievement to self-efficacy (pp. 25-46, Chapter viii, 147 Pages).
