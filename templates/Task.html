Monitoring

In operational performance terms, self-monitoring usually involves keeping a physical record of one’s academic performance (written logs, wrist counter tallies, and audio- or videotape recordings for example). Such records can provide information about the quality and the outcomes of a student’s academic performance. Self-monitoring can help students discriminate between effective and ineffective performance, reveal the inadequacy of a learning strategy, and prompt the students to find a more suitable one, and also foster reflective thinking.

To enhance your self-monitoring skills, these are the four things you can do:

  1. Decide whether self-monitoring is necessary: change or improve some aspect of your approach to academic learning
  2. Determine what to monitor: specify a problem area and behaviors
  3. Decide on a means for monitoring the behavior: e.g. log for recording
  4. Decide how to evaluate and react to the information gather through self-monitoring

Key techniques: keep learning records, track learning status

Organizing

Organization refers to the ability of a learner to select the appropriate information and organize their thoughts during a learning process. For example, through homework assignments, students will read and summarize textual passages followed by a quiz based on these readings. Below are some selected learning strategies than can be considered:

  1. Clarifying difficulties encountered. Slowing down to read more carefully and checking back and rereading when text meaning is unclear.
  2. Finding the main idea. Analyzing passages, such as a paragraph, and identifying the main idea that is being discussed.
  3. Summarization. Paring down passages to their core meaning by eliminating trivial or redundant information, substituting superordinate terms for lists of items, integrating a series of events with a superordinate action term, and inventing a topic sentence.
  4. Relating text to prior knowledge. Relating ideas in text to previously stored information, often in the form of analogies, examples, extensions, and comparisons.

Key techniques: summarize, list main ideas, highlight key concepts

Elaborating

Elaboration refers to the ability to connect prior knowledge with new information with the objective of remembering the new material. Below are some selected learning strategies than can be considered:

  1. Recording strategies. Be selective about what you include in your notes. Write down only main ideas and supporting facts. Listen for summary statements and write them down. Listen for signal words, for example, “there are four reasons.” Trying to write everything down burdens a student with trivial and often results in the student’s not being able to keep up with the lecture.

The type of visual structure must be chosen on the basis of the material, for example, a lecture comparing and contrasting religions can be captured using a two dimensional matrix.
